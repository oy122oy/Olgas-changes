Proportional Reasoning

In this lesson, we explore proportional reasoning — how quantities change in relation to each other. By the end, you'll be able to spot, solve, and survive any AQA proportion question.

Welcome to the world of proportion, where maths basically asks: “if this changes… what happens to that?”

It’s like adjusting the volume on your phone — everything scales up or down. Except here, instead of music, it’s numbers. Slightly less fun, but still useful.


Direct Proportion

Direct proportion means:

as one thing increases, the other increases too

Example:

  • More items → more cost
  • More distance → more time (at same speed)

If 5 pens cost £10, then:

  • 1 pen costs £2
  • 10 pens cost £20

Everything scales nicely. Like resizing a photo… except less blurry.


The formula:

y = kx

k is the constant of proportionality (basically the “per one” value).

Find k first. Always. Skipping this step is like trying to bake a cake without checking the recipe. It might work… but it probably won’t.


Inverse Proportion

Now things get spicy.

as one increases, the other decreases

Example:

  • More workers → less time
  • Higher speed → less travel time

Formula:

y = k / x

So if you double x, y halves. Maths suddenly becomes very dramatic.


AQA Exam Tips

  • Always identify: direct or inverse
  • Find k early
  • Write units clearly

Mixing up direct and inverse proportion in an exam is like putting salt in your tea. Technically possible… but deeply concerning.
