Quadratics

In this lesson, we explore quadratic equations and how to solve them using factoring, completing the square, and the quadratic formula. By the end, you will be able to identify, graph, and solve any quadratic equation with confidence. Take your time, pause the video whenever you need, and make sure you understand each step before moving on.


Welcome to the weird and wonderful world of quadratics. Yes, the word sounds like a robot sneezed while doing maths homework, but stay with me.

A quadratic equation usually looks like this:

ax² + bx + c = 0

Where:

  • a, b, and c are numbers
  • x² means x multiplied by itself (x × x)
  • The x² is the important part — if you see that, you’ve spotted a quadratic

Congratulations. You are now officially a Quadratic Detective. Imagine yourself wearing a trench coat and investigating suspicious-looking numbers.


When you draw the graph of a quadratic, you don’t get a boring straight line. Instead you get a parabola, which is a smooth curve that looks like:

  • a banana
  • a skateboard ramp
  • or the path a confused pigeon might fly if it suddenly remembered it left the oven on

AQA exam questions often ask where the graph crosses the x-axis. These values are called the roots or solutions.

Think of the graph like a trampoline. The points where it touches the ground are the roots.


Method 1: Factorising

Example:

x² + 5x + 6 = 0

We look for two numbers that:

  • multiply to make 6
  • add to make 5

The numbers are 2 and 3.

So we rewrite the equation like this:

(x + 2)(x + 3) = 0

Now we use the golden rule:

If two things multiply to make zero, one of them must be zero.

  • x + 2 = 0 → x = −2
  • x + 3 = 0 → x = −3

That’s it. Two solutions found. Like discovering two secret exits from a maths escape room.


Method 2: The Quadratic Formula

Sometimes factorising refuses to cooperate, like a stubborn cat that won’t get in its carrier. That’s when the quadratic formula appears like a maths superhero:

x = (-b ± √(b² − 4ac)) / 2a

The part inside the square root is called the discriminant:

b² − 4ac

This tells you what kind of answers you’ll get:

  • positive → two solutions
  • zero → one solution
  • negative → no real solutions

Final AQA Survival Tips

  • Try factorising first because it’s usually faster
  • Use the quadratic formula if the numbers look ugly
  • Always write both answers

Forgetting the second answer in an AQA exam is like ordering pizza and only eating the crust. Technically possible, but deeply upsetting.
